# Future of ID — First Five Minutes

## Purpose

This document defines the intended first five minutes of the entire Future of ID product experience, not just the first module.

It acts as an experience storyboard. It describes what a learner should think, feel, notice, and do when they first encounter the product across the homepage, dashboard, orientation, and early module surfaces.

This is not a UI specification, wireframe, or React implementation guide. It should guide future Figma, React, AI, and Reverse Build decisions by clarifying the early learner experience.

## Document Ownership

This document owns the first-impression learner storyboard for Future of ID.

It should guide homepage, dashboard, orientation, and early module decisions by defining the intended emotional and cognitive progression of the learner's first few minutes.

Use this document when deciding whether early product surfaces help learners understand what Future of ID is, why it matters, where they can go, and why they may want to continue.

## Success Criteria

By the end of the first five minutes:

- The learner understands this is not a traditional LMS.
- The learner understands the product is self-directed and open access.
- The learner feels curious, not overwhelmed.
- The learner sees credibility and professional relevance.
- The learner has a clear next step without being forced into one path.
- The learner wants to explore at least one capability.

## Learner Mindset Before Arrival

Before arriving, the learner may assume:

- "This is probably another AI course."
- "This might be another content-heavy professional development site."
- "I want useful ideas, not theory for theory's sake."
- "I may only have a few minutes to explore."
- "I want to know if this is worth my time."

The first five minutes should gently challenge those assumptions by showing that Future of ID is practical, self-directed, professionally credible, and built around exploration rather than required progression.

## Minute 0–1 — First Impression

Learner thought:

"This does not feel like another online course."

### Learner Thoughts

- This feels more like a modern learning product than a course shell.
- The topic feels relevant to where instructional design is going.
- I can tell this was designed intentionally.

### Desired Emotion

Interested, alert, and pleasantly surprised.

### Page Responsibility

The first surface should communicate a clear product promise: Future of ID provides a reusable system for intentional capability development and demonstrates it through Bradley's current pathway.

It should quickly separate the product from generic AI training, static articles, slide-like courseware, or corporate LMS patterns.

### Success Indicators

- The learner understands the product has a point of view.
- The learner sees Bradley's seven-capability demonstration as useful without mistaking it for a universal framework.
- The learner senses that the experience is polished and professionally relevant.

### Design Implications

- Lead with clarity and relevance before detail.
- Avoid vague AI hype, dense explanations, or generic course language.
- Make the first next step visible without making it feel mandatory.

## Minute 1–2 — Curiosity

Learner thought:

"What kind of product is this?"

### Learner Thoughts

- Is this a course, a portfolio, a toolkit, or a guided exploration?
- What can I do here besides read?
- Can I choose what interests me?

### Desired Emotion

Curious and oriented.

### Page Responsibility

The product should reveal that Future of ID is a guided, self-paced learning experience with theory, practice, Reverse Build, reflection, and AI-supported feedback.

It should make exploration feel available immediately.

### Success Indicators

- The learner can describe the basic experience model.
- The learner understands that capabilities are available to explore.
- The learner sees that interaction and Reverse Build are part of the value.

### Design Implications

- Explain the experience model in plain language.
- Show enough structure to create confidence, but not so much that the learner feels trapped in a syllabus.
- Present navigation as invitation, not enforcement.

## Minute 2–3 — Credibility

Learner thought:

"This was built by someone who understands instructional design."

### Learner Thoughts

- This connects AI and future-facing ideas to real L&D work.
- The product understands instructional design as more than course production.
- The language feels grounded, not trend-driven.

### Desired Emotion

Trust, professional respect, and confidence.

### Page Responsibility

The product should show instructional credibility through clear framing, relevant capability language, practical implications, and transparent design reasoning.

Credibility should come from specificity, structure, and usefulness rather than credential display alone.

### Success Indicators

- The learner sees familiar L&D concerns reflected in the product.
- The learner understands that Future of ID expands traditional instructional design rather than dismissing it.
- The learner believes the experience may help them make better design decisions.

### Design Implications

- Use precise instructional design language without becoming academic.
- Connect future-ready capabilities to practical workplace decisions.
- Surface enough rationale to build trust, especially through Reverse Build cues.

## Minute 3–4 — Participation

Learner thought:

"I can start exploring immediately."

### Learner Thoughts

- I do not need an account to begin.
- I do not have to complete a prerequisite before exploring.
- I can choose the capability that matters to me.

### Desired Emotion

Agency, momentum, and relief.

### Page Responsibility

The product should make self-directed participation obvious. Learners should understand that modules are internally guided, but the overall course path is open.

Nothing should suggest that content is locked, hidden, or dependent on saved progress.

### Success Indicators

- The learner can enter a module, dashboard, orientation, or capability without confusion.
- The learner understands progress is informative rather than restrictive.
- The learner feels invited to act, not pushed through a required sequence.

### Design Implications

- Make available paths visible and understandable.
- Avoid lock language, prerequisite language, or completion-gated messaging.
- Provide orientation without making orientation feel like a gate.

## Minute 4–5 — Commitment

Learner thought:

"I want to see what is inside the next capability."

### Learner Thoughts

- This looks useful enough to continue.
- I want to inspect how this experience was designed.
- I can see how at least one capability connects to my work.

### Desired Emotion

Motivated, curious, and ready for a next step.

### Page Responsibility

The product should create a meaningful reason to continue, whether the learner chooses orientation, dashboard, a specific module, an AI interaction, or a Reverse Build surface.

The commitment should come from perceived value, not pressure.

### Success Indicators

- The learner chooses a next step voluntarily.
- The learner wants to explore at least one capability.
- The learner sees practical value in returning.

### Design Implications

- Make next steps specific and meaningful.
- Connect capabilities to practical questions learners may already have.
- Preserve the feeling that the learner is choosing their path.

## Page-Level Roles In The First Five Minutes

### Homepage

- Psychological job: Answer "Why should I care?"
- What the learner should feel: Interested, oriented, and confident that the product has a useful point of view.
- What the page must avoid: Generic AI hype, vague course promises, or overexplaining the full architecture before the learner is ready.

### About Page

- Psychological job: Answer "Why should I trust this?"
- What the learner should feel: Reassured that the product is grounded in real instructional design practice and thoughtful system-building.
- What the page must avoid: Becoming a biography that distracts from learner value or relying on claims without showing product reasoning.

### Dashboard

- Psychological job: Answer "Where do I want to go?"
- What the learner should feel: In control of an open capability landscape.
- What the page must avoid: Looking like a locked course tracker, compliance dashboard, or linear requirement list.

### Orientation

- Psychological job: Answer "How does this experience work?"
- What the learner should feel: Grounded and ready to explore.
- What the page must avoid: Acting like a gate, prerequisite, or long explanation that delays meaningful participation.

### Module Page

- Psychological job: Answer "What capability am I exploring?"
- What the learner should feel: Focused, respected, and able to understand why this capability matters.
- What the page must avoid: Assuming the learner arrived from a prior module or burying practical value under too much upfront theory.

## What Must Not Happen

- It must not feel like a corporate LMS.
- It must not depend on login, database persistence, or permanent local storage.
- It must not imply content is locked behind completion.
- It must not overwhelm learners with too much explanation too early.
- It must not force a single path.
- It must not use vague AI hype.
- It must not hide the practical value.

## Experience Checklist

Use this checklist before designing or implementing first-five-minute surfaces:

- Does this help the learner understand where they are?
- Does this create curiosity without confusion?
- Does this provide a clear next step without forcing a path?
- Does this make Future of ID feel premium and professional?
- Does this support self-directed exploration?
- Does this avoid dependence on persistent saved progress?
- Does this make the learner want to continue?
